Delivery scams impersonate postal services or courier companies, claiming you have a package waiting or need to pay a small customs fee. These scams exploit our expectation of online shopping deliveries and use small payment amounts to seem legitimate.

üéØ Common Scam Tactics

üí∞ Small Amount Trick

Scammers ask for small fees like $2.80 or $3.50 for "customs clearance" or "delivery charges." The amount seems too small to be worth scamming, making victims more likely to pay without verification.

‚è∞ Countdown Pressure

Fake notifications include countdown timers (e.g., "Pay within 24 hours") to create urgency and prevent you from thinking critically or verifying the message.

üìã Realistic Tracking Numbers

Scam messages include fake tracking numbers that look authentic but can't be verified on official postal service websites.

üîó Phishing Payment Pages

Links lead to fake payment pages designed to steal your credit card information, not just the small fee amount.

üö® Typical Scam SMS:

Why This Works:
  • Uses legitimate company name (SingPost)
  • Includes tracking-style number
  • Small amount seems believable
  • Creates urgency with 24-hour deadline
  • Threatens package return (loss aversion)
  • Many people shop online and expect packages

üõ°Ô∏è How to Protect Yourself

1 Verify Tracking Numbers

Always check tracking numbers on the official postal service website or app. Real tracking numbers work on official platforms.

2 Don't Click SMS Links

Never click payment links in SMS or emails. If you need to pay customs fees, do so at the collection point or through official channels.

3 Check If You're Expecting a Package

Review your online orders. If you haven't ordered anything, you won't receive a delivery. Scammers send thousands of messages hoping to hit someone expecting a package.

4 Be Skeptical of Small Fees

Legitimate customs fees are usually higher and come with official documentation. Small fees like $2-5 are common scam amounts designed to seem insignificant.

5 Contact Customer Service

Call the official customer service hotline (e.g., SingPost 1605) to verify any delivery issues or payment requests.

6 Use Official Apps

Download official postal service apps from legitimate app stores. Check all delivery notifications within the app, not from external messages.

7 Look for Red Flags

Poor grammar, generic greetings, shortened URLs, and urgent deadlines are all warning signs of scam messages.

üí° Golden Rule

If you haven't ordered anything, you won't receive a legitimate delivery notification. When in doubt, ignore the message and check directly with the postal service through official channels. The "small fee" is just bait to steal your payment information.

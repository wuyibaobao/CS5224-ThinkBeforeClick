University tuition scams target students with fake refund notifications claiming MOE tuition grant adjustments or billing errors resulted in overpayments. These scams impersonate legitimate universities to steal personal information, banking details, or Singpass credentials.

üéØ Why Students Are Targeted

Students Are Vulnerable Because:

  • Financial Stress: Many students struggle financially and are eager for refunds
  • Less Experience: Younger students may have less experience identifying scams
  • Trust in Institutions: Students trust communications claiming to be from their university
  • Complex Fee Structures: Tuition grants and subsidies are complicated, making "errors" believable
  • International Students: Less familiar with local scam tactics and official procedures
  • Digital Natives: Comfortable clicking links and responding to emails quickly

‚úÖ How to Protect Yourself

üõ°Ô∏è Student Safety Guide

1. Access Student Portal Directly

Always log into your university's official student portal by typing the URL directly. Never click links in emails claiming to be from your university.

2. Check Official Portal First

All legitimate refunds, credits, or fee adjustments appear in your official student account. Check there before responding to any email.

3. Contact Finance Office

Call your university's Office of Financial Services using the number from the official website. They can confirm any refund claims.

4. Verify Email Sender

Real university emails come from official domains (e.g., @nus.edu.sg, @ntu.edu.sg). Hover over sender addresses to verify - scammers use similar-looking domains.

5. Universities Don't Email Refunds

Legitimate refunds are processed automatically to your registered bank account and shown in your student portal, not announced via random emails.

6. Never Share Singpass Details

Universities will NEVER ask for your Singpass password or OTP. Anyone requesting these is a scammer.

7. Don't Provide Banking Information

Universities already have your bank details from enrollment. Any request to "update" or "verify" is suspicious.

8. Be Skeptical of Forfeiture Threats

Real universities don't "forfeit" refunds owed to students. This is a pressure tactic used by scammers.

9. Check for Personalization

Real university communications include your full name and complete student ID, not generic greetings or partial IDs.

10. Report to IT Security

Forward suspicious emails to your university's IT security team (e.g., itcare@nus.edu.sg). This helps protect other students.

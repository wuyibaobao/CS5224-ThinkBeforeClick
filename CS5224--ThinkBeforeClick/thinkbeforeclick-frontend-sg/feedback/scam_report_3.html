Tax refund scams impersonate government tax authorities (like IRAS in Singapore or IRS in the US) claiming you're owed a tax refund. These scams exploit people's trust in government agencies and the appeal of unexpected money to steal personal information and banking details.

üé≠ How the Scam Works

1

Initial Contact

You receive an official-looking email or SMS claiming you're eligible for a tax refund due to "overpayment" or "system recalculation."

2

Attractive Amount

The refund amount (e.g., $843.50) seems specific and believable - not too large to be suspicious, but significant enough to be worth claiming.

3

Urgency Created

Message includes a deadline (e.g., "claim by October 25") with threats that unclaimed refunds will be "forfeited."

4

Phishing Link

You're directed to click a link to "claim" your refund, leading to a fake website that looks identical to the real tax authority portal.

5

Data Theft

The fake site asks for personal details, tax reference numbers, bank account information, or even Singpass credentials - all of which are stolen.

üîç How to Verify Tax Refunds Safely

1

Access myTax Portal Directly

Type mytax.iras.gov.sg directly into your browser. Never click links in emails or SMS claiming to be from IRAS.

2

Check Your Notice of Assessment

Log into myTax Portal and view your official Notice of Assessment (NOA). Real refunds will be clearly stated there.

3

Call Official Hotline

Contact IRAS directly at 1800 356 8300. Use only the official number from the IRAS website, not numbers in suspicious messages.

4

Check Email Domain

Official IRAS emails come from @iras.gov.sg domain. Hover over the sender address to verify - scammers often use similar-looking domains.

5

Review Your Tax History

Think about whether you've filed taxes recently and if an overpayment is plausible. Unexpected refunds are often scams.

6

Never Share Singpass Details

IRAS will never ask for your Singpass password or OTP. These are your personal credentials that should never be shared.

üéØ Why Tax Scams Are Effective

1. Authority Trust: People trust government agencies and are less likely to question official-looking messages.

2. Financial Appeal: The promise of receiving money makes people act quickly without verification.

3. Tax Season Timing: Scammers increase activity during tax filing periods when people expect communications from tax authorities.

4. Complexity: Tax laws are complicated, so people believe there could be errors or recalculations they weren't aware of.

5. Fear of Loss: Threats that refunds will be "forfeited" trigger loss aversion psychology.

üí° Golden Rule for Tax Refunds

IRAS will never send refund notifications via email with external payment links. All legitimate tax refunds are processed automatically to your registered bank account and clearly shown in your myTax Portal. When in doubt, log in directly to myTax Portal or call the official IRAS hotline. The extra few minutes of verification could save you from identity theft and financial loss.
